5 Credit and liquidity risks

5.1 Dynamic credit risk models: Why?

  • Compute Values-at-Risk of portfolios of credit-sensitive instruments.
  • Extract term structures of default probabilities from credit spreads (CDS).
  • Compute expected excess returns of defaultable bonds.
  • Price exotic credit derivatives.
  • Academic perspective: Understanding the pricing of credit risk.

5.2 Notations

  • The information of the investor at date \(t\) is \(\underline{w_t} = (w_t',w_{t-1}',\dots, w_1 ') '\), where \(w_t = (y_t',d_t')'\):
  • \(y_t\) is a \(n_y\)-dimensional vector of common factors,
  • \(d_t = (d_{1, t} , \dots, d_{E, t}) '\) a \(E\)-dimensional vector of binary variables representing the possible default of entities \(e \in \{1, \dots,E\}\):

\(d_{e, t} = 1 \Leftrightarrow\) entity \(e\) is in default status at time \(t\).

\(d_{e, t} = 0 \Leftrightarrow\) entity \(e\) is not in default status at time \(t\).

  • \(w_t\) is a \(K\)-dimensional vector \(\Rightarrow\) \(K = n_y + E\).
  • We assume that the process \(\{ w_t \}\) is Markov \(\Rightarrow\) Its historical (\(\mathbb{P}\)) dynamics is defined by the conditional densities: \[ f (y_t | w_{t-1}) \mbox{ and }p ( d_t | y_t, w_{t-1} ). \]

Hypothesis 5.1 (absorbing defaults) The default event is an absorbing state: \(p_e ( 0 | y_t, w_{t-1}, d_{e,t-1}=1 ) = 0\).

  • Survival probability: \[\begin{equation} p_e ( 0 | y_t, w_{t-1}, d_{e,t-1}=0 ) = \exp[-\underbrace{\lambda_e ( y_t, w_{t-1} )}_{\color{blue}{\mbox{default intensity}}}].\tag{5.1} \end{equation}\]

5.3 Pricing under standard assumptions

Hypothesis 2.1 (non-systemic entity) \(\{d_t\}\) does not Granger-cause \(\{y_t\}\), in the sense that: \[ f(y_t|w_{t-1}) = f(y_t|y_{t-1}). \]

Hypothesis 2.3 (unpriced credit events) The default events (or credit events) are not priced, in the sense that: \[\begin{equation*} M_{t-1, t}( w_t, w_{t-1}) = M_{t-1, t}( y_t, y_{t-1}) . \end{equation*}\]

  • Short-term risk-free rate: The previous two assumptions notably imply that \(r_t\) (\(=-\log\mathbb{E}_t(M_{t,t+1})\)) depends on \(\underline{y_t}\) (but not on \(d_t\)).

  • We start with a presentation of the classical credit-risk framework.

  • Let us first focus on a single entity (\(d_t \equiv d_{1,t}\)), alive on date \(t\) (\(d_t=0\)).

  • Consider the date-\(t\) price of a bond issued by the defaultable entity; residual maturity \(h\) and zero recovery rate (0 payoff in case of default 0): \[ B(t,h) = \mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}[\exp(-r_t-\dots-r_{t+h-1})(1-d_{t+h})]. \]

  • Conditioning w.r.t. \(\underline{y_{t+h}}\), this price is given by: \[\begin{eqnarray*} && \mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}[\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\{\exp(-r_t-\dots-r_{t+h-1})(1-d_{t+h})|\underline{y_{t+h}}\}]\\ &=& \mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}[\exp(-r_t-\dots-r_{t+h-1})\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\{(1-d_{t+h})|\underline{y_{t+h}}\}]\\ &=& \mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}[\exp(-r_t-\dots-r_{t+h-1})\mathbb{Q}\{d_{t+h}=0|\underline{y_{t+h}}\}]\\ &=& \mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[\exp(-r_t-\dots-r_{t+h-1})\prod_{j=1}^h\mathbb{Q}\{d_{t+j}=0|d_{t+j-1}=0,\underline{y_{t+h}}\}\right]. \end{eqnarray*}\]

  • Because Granger and Sims non-causalities are equivalent, we have: \[ \mathbb{Q}\{d_{t+j}=0|d_{t+j-1}=0,\underline{y_{t+{\color{red}h}}}\} = \underbrace{\mathbb{Q}\{d_{t+j}=0|d_{t+j-1}=0,\underline{y_{t+{\color{red}j}}}\}}_{=\exp(-\lambda_{t+j}^{\mathbb{Q}})}. \] \[\begin{equation} \Rightarrow B(t,h) = \mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[\exp\left(-r_t-\dots-r_{t+h-1}- \lambda_{t+1}^{\mathbb{Q}} - \dots - \lambda_{t+h}^{\mathbb{Q}}\right)\right].\tag{5.2} \end{equation}\]

  • Now, what is the relationship between \(\lambda_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\) and \(\lambda_t^{\mathbb{P}}\)? (\(\lambda_t^{\mathbb{P}}\equiv\lambda_t\); default intensity introduced in Eq. (5.1)

  • We have (Eq. (2.5)): \[\begin{equation} f^{\mathbb{Q}}(d_t,y_t|d_{t-1},y_{t-1}) = \exp(r_{t-1})M_{t-1,t} f^{\mathbb{P}}(d_t,y_t|d_{t-1},y_{t-1}).\tag{5.3} \end{equation}\]

  • Using that \(M_{t-1,t}\) does not depend on \(\underline{d_t}\) (see Remark XXXX), we obtain, by integrating both sides w.r.t. \(d_t\): \[\begin{eqnarray} f^{\mathbb{Q}}(y_t|d_{t-1},y_{t-1}) &=& \exp(r_{t-1})M_{t-1,t} f^{\mathbb{P}}(y_t|d_{t-1},y_{t-1})\nonumber\\ &=& \exp(r_{t-1})M_{t-1,t} \underbrace{f^{\mathbb{P}}(y_t|y_{t-1})}_{\mbox{by hypothesis}}. \tag{5.4} \end{eqnarray}\]

  • By Bayes, we have: \[ f^{\mathbb{Q}}(d_t|d_{t-1},y_t,y_{t-1}) = \frac{f^{\mathbb{Q}}(d_t,y_t|d_{t-1},y_{t-1})}{f^{\mathbb{Q}}(y_t|d_{t-1},y_{t-1})}. \]

  • Using Eq. (5.3) (numerator) and Eq. (5.4) (denominator), we get: \[\begin{eqnarray*} && f^{\mathbb{Q}}(d_t|d_{t-1},y_t,y_{t-1}) = f^{\mathbb{P}}(d_t|d_{t-1},y_t,y_{t-1}) \Rightarrow \boxed{\lambda_t^{\mathbb{Q}} = \lambda_t^{\mathbb{P}}.} \end{eqnarray*}\]

  • Using this in Eq. (5.2), we obtain that, under Hypotheses 2.1 and 2.3, and for a zero recovery rate: \[\begin{equation} \boxed{B(t,h) = \mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[\exp\left(-r_t-\dots-r_{t+h-1}- \lambda_{t+1} - \dots - \lambda_{t+h}\right)\right].}\tag{5.5} \end{equation}\]

  • Although \(\mathbb{P}\) and \(\mathbb{Q}\) intensities are the same functions of \(y_t\) and \(y_{t-1}\), their historical and risk-neutral dynamics are in general different since \(y_t\)’s \(\mathbb{P}\) and \(\mathbb{Q}\) dynamics are different.

  • Eq. (5.5) rewrites: \[\begin{equation} B(t,h) = \mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[\exp\left(-\tilde{r}_t-\dots-\tilde{r}_{t+h-1}\right)\right],\tag{5.6} \end{equation}\] where \(\tilde{r}_t = r_t + \lambda_{t+1}\).

  • Eq. (5.6) is reminiscent of Eq. (1.7), where the risk-free short-term rate \(r_t\) is replaced by the credit-adjusted short-term rate \(\tilde{r}_t\).

  • If both \(r_t\) and \(\lambda_t\) are affine in \(y_t\), where the latter is an affine process, then bond prices are easily computed by means of recursive formulas (as was the case in the context of Eq. (1.7), see Example 1.15.

  • In this case, \(B(t,h)\) is exponential affine in \(y_t\), that is, it is of the form: \[\begin{equation} B(t,h) = \exp({A^d_h}'y_t + B^d_h)\quad (say).\tag{5.7} \end{equation}\]

  • The previous framework can be adapted in order to accommodate non-zero recovery rates.

  • In this framework, the default intensity will be replaced by a pseudo, or recovery-adjusted default intensity \(\tilde\lambda_t\).

  • Recovery of Market Value [RMV] modelling convention: In case of default, the recovery payoff is a fraction \(\zeta \in [0,1]\) Recovery Rate, (RR) of the price that would have prevailed, absent the default.

  • Sketch if the proof of Eq. (5.9)

  • If \(d_t=0\) (entity alive on date \(t\)), we then have: \[\begin{eqnarray*} B(t,h) &=& \exp(-r_t)\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}} [ d_{t+1} \zeta B(t+1,h-1) + (1-d_{t+1})B(t+1,h-1)]\\ &=& \exp(-r_t)\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}} [\{1 - d_{t+1} (1-\zeta)\} B(t+1,h-1) ]. \end{eqnarray*}\]

  • Defining the recovery-adjusted default intensity \(\tilde\lambda_{t+1}\) through \[\begin{eqnarray} \exp(-\tilde\lambda_{t+1}) &=& \mathbb{E} [\{1 - d_{t+1} (1-\zeta)\}|\underline{y_{t+1}},d_t=0] \nonumber\\ &=& 1 - (1-\zeta) (1-\exp(-\lambda_{t+1})),\tag{5.8} \end{eqnarray}\] one obtains: \[\begin{eqnarray*} B(t,h) &=& \mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}} [\exp(-r_t-\tilde\lambda_{t+1}) B(t+1,h-1) ], \end{eqnarray*}\] and, by recursion: \[\begin{eqnarray} B(t,h) &=& \mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}} [\exp(-r_t-\tilde\lambda_{t+1}-\dots -r_{t+h-1}-\tilde\lambda_{t+h})].\tag{5.9} \end{eqnarray}\]

  • This is formalized in the following proposition.

Proposition 5.1 (Bond pricing in the RMV classical framework) Under Hypotheses 2.1 and 2.3, and if, in case of default on date \(t+i\), the recovery payment is \(\zeta \widetilde{B}(t+i,h-i)\), where: * the pseudo-price \(\widetilde{B}(t,h)\) is given by: \[ \widetilde{B}(t,h) = \mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}} [\exp(-r_{t}-\tilde\lambda_{t+1}-\dots -r_{t+h-1}-\tilde\lambda_{t+h}], h\ge 1, \] with \(B(t,0)=1\). * the pseudo-intensity \(\tilde\lambda_{t}\) is defined in Eq. ((5.8)), * \(\zeta \in [0,1]\), then \[\begin{equation} B(t,h) = (1-d_t)\widetilde{B}(t,h).\tag{5.10} \end{equation}\]

Proof. Relation (5.10) is true for \(B(t+h, 0)\) since \(\widetilde{B}(t+h, 0) = 1\) and \(B(t+h, 0) = 1 - d_{t+h}\). Assuming that \(B(t+i+1, h-i-1) = (1 - d_{t+i+1} ) \widetilde{B}(t+i+1, h-i-1)\) (which is valid for \(i=h-1\)), we get: \[\begin{eqnarray*} B(t+i, h-i) &=& \left(1 - d_{t+i} \right) \mathbb{E}_{t+i}^{\mathbb{Q}} \left\{ \exp(- r_{t+i}) \left[ \left(1 - d_{t+i+1} \right) \widetilde{B}(t+i+1, h-i-1) \right. \right. \\ && \left. \left. + d_{t+i+1}\zeta\widetilde{B}(t+i+1, h-i-1) \right] \right\}, \end{eqnarray*}\] since, in case of no default at date \(t+i\), the value of the bond at date \(t+i+1\) is either \(\zeta B(t+i+1, h-i-1) = \zeta \widetilde{B}(t+i+1, h-i-1)\) if default happens, and \(B(t+i+1, h-i-1) = \widetilde{B}(t+i+1, h-i-1)\) otherwise. Hypotheses 2.1 and 2.3 imply that \(\widetilde{B}(t+i+1, h-i-1)\) does not depend on \(d_{t+i+1}\) and taking first the conditional expectation given \(w_{t+i}\) and \(y_{t+i+1}\) we obtain: \[\begin{equation} \begin{array}{lll} B(t+i, h-i) &=& \left(1 - d_{t+i} \right) \mathbb{E}_{t+i}^{\mathbb{Q}} \left\{ \exp(- r_{t+i}) \widetilde{B}(t+i+1, h-i-1) \times \right. \\ && \quad\left. \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}} \left[ \left(1 - d_{t+i+1} \right) + \zeta d_{t+i+1} | y_{t+i+1}, w_{t+i} \right] \right\} . \end{array}\tag{5.11} \end{equation}\] Using the definition of \(\widetilde{\lambda}_{t}\) (Eq. (5.8)) we get: \[\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{lll} B(t+i, h-i) &=& \left(1 - d_{t+i} \right) \mathbb{E}_{t+i}^{\mathbb{Q}} \left\{ \exp \left( - r_{t+i} - \widetilde{\lambda}_{t+i+1} \right) \widetilde{B}(t+i+1, h-i-1) \right\}\\ &=& \left(1 - d_{t+i} \right) \widetilde{B}(t+i, h-i) . \end{array} \end{equation*}\] Therefore it is also true for \(i=0\).

  • Again, if \(r_t\) and \(\tilde\lambda_t\) are affine combinations of an affine process \(y_t\), then (exponential affine) bond prices are easily computed by means of recursive formulas, see on next slide.

Hypothesis 5.2 (Affine process) The process \(\{ y_t \}\) is affine under the \(\mathbb{Q}\) measure: \[\begin{equation} \begin{array}{lll} \varphi^{\mathbb{Q}}_{y, t-1} (u_y) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}} \left[\exp( u_y ' y_t ) | \underline{y_{t-1}} \right] = \exp \left[ a^{\mathbb{Q}}_{y} (u_{y}) ' y_{t-1} + b^{\mathbb{Q}}_{y} (u_{y}) \right], \end{array}\tag{5.12} \end{equation}\] and \(r_t\) and \(\widetilde{\lambda}_{e, t}\) are affine in \((y_t, y_{t-1})\), that is (say): \[\begin{eqnarray} r_{t} &=& \omega_0 + \omega_{1} ' y_{t} \\ \widetilde{\lambda}_{e, t} &=& \kappa_{0} + \kappa_{1}' y_t. \end{eqnarray}\]

Proposition 5.2 (Standard affine bond pricing, under RMV convention) Under the Assumptions of Proposition 5.1 and under Hypothesis @ref(hyp:A6}, we have, for \(h \ge 1\): \[\begin{equation} B(t, h) = \left(1 - d_{t} \right)\exp(- h[\omega_0 + \kappa_{0}] - \omega_{1} ' y_{t} + A_h'y_t + B_h),\tag{5.13} \end{equation}\] where \(A_h\) and \(B_h\) are given by the following recursive equations: \[\begin{equation} \left\{ \begin{array}{ccl} A_{h} &=& a(u_{h} + A_{h-1}), \\ B_{h} &=& b(u_{h} + A_{h-1}) + B_{h-1}, \\ A_{0} &=& 0,\quad B_{0} = 0, \end{array} \right.\tag{5.14} \end{equation}\] with \(u_1 = -\kappa_{1}\) and \(u_i = -(\kappa_{1} + \omega_{1})\) for \(i>1\).

Proof. The results directly follows from Proposition 5.1 and from Remark XXX.

  • What about when different entities are considered (\(E = 2\))?
  • Same pricing formulas are obtained under the assumption of no-contagion (Hypothesis 2.2).

:::{,hypothesis #A2 name=“no contagion”} There is no instantaneous or lagged contagion between entities, i.e.: \[\begin{equation*} p ( d_t | y_t, w_{t-1} ) = p_1 ( d_{1, t} | y_t, y_{t-1}, d_{1, t-1}) \times p_2 ( d_{2, t} | y_t, y_{t-1}, d_{2, t-1}) . \end{equation*}\] :::

  • If Hypothesis 2.2 is not satisfied, e.g. if: \[\begin{eqnarray*} p(d_t|y_t, w_{t-1} ) &=& p_1 ( d_{1, t} | y_t, y_{t-1}, d_{1, t-1}) \times \\ && p_2 ( d_{2, t} | y_t, y_{t-1}, \color{blue}{d_{1, t}}, \color{blue}{d_{1, t-1}}, d_{2, t-1}). \end{eqnarray*}\] (contagion effect from e = 1 towards e = 2), then:
  1. Proposition 5.1 is still valid for both entities;
  2. In an affine framework, the computation of \(\widetilde{B}_1 (t, h)\) (and \(B_1 (t, h)\)) is straightforward;
  3. but formulas for \(\widetilde{B}_2 (t, h)\) and \(B_2 (t, h)\) are not explicit anymore even if \(\widetilde{\lambda}_{2, t}\) is affine in \((y_t, y_{t-1}, d_{1, t}, d_{1, t-1})\).

Proposition 5.3 (Defaultable bond pricing in affine frameworks) Under Hypotheses 5.1, 2.1, 2.3 and 2.2, the risk-neutral (\(\mathbb{Q}\), say) dynamics is such that: \[ f^{\mathbb{Q}} ( y_t | w_{t-1}) = f^{\mathbb{Q}} ( y_t | y_{t-1}) \propto M_{t-1, t}( y_t, y_{t-1}) f (y_t | y_{t-1}) \] (exogeneity of \(y_t\) preserved under \(\mathbb{Q}\)) and

\[ p_t^{\mathbb{Q}} ( d_{t} | y_t, w_{t-1} ) = p_1 ( d_{1, t} | y_t, y_{t-1}, d_{1, t-1} ) \times p_2 ( d_{2, t} | y_t, y_{t-1}, d_{2, t-1}), \] (absence of contagion preserved under \(\mathbb{Q}\)).

Denoting by \(\lambda^{\mathbb{Q}}_e \left( y_t, y_{t-1} \right) \equiv - \log \left[ p_e^{\mathbb{Q}} ( 0 | y_t, y_{t-1}, 0 ) \right]\) the risk-neutral default intensity of entity \(e\), it comes that: \[ \lambda^{\mathbb{Q}}_e \left( y_t, y_{t-1} \right) = \lambda_e \left( y_t, y_{t-1} \right) \] ({}).

Proof. The \(\mathbb{Q}\) conditional density of \(w_t\) given \(w_{t-1}\), namely \(f^{\mathbb{Q}} (y_t | w_{t-1}) p^{\mathbb{Q}} ( d_t | y_t, w_{t-1} )\), is proportional to \(M_{t-1, t}( w_t, w_{t-1}) f (y_t | w_{t-1}) p ( d_t | y_t, w_{t-1} )\), and thus proportional to \(M_{t-1, t}( y_t, y_{t-1}) f (y_t | y_{t-1}) p_1 ( d_{1, t} | y_t, y_{t-1}, d_{1, t-1}) p_2 ( d_{2, t} | y_t, y_{t-1}, d_{2, t-1})\). The result follows immediately.

5.4 Pricing Illiquid Bonds

  • A very similar approach can be used to price illiquid assets.
  • The idea consists in replacing the Loss Given Default (\(LGD = 1-RR^{(e)}\)) by a Loss Given Liquidity Shock (\(1-\theta_\ell^{(e)}\)).
  • Structural interpretation building on Ericsson and Renault (2006) (see AppendixXXX).
  • Novel binary variable: \(\ell_t\) = liquidity shocks

\(\ell_{t}=1\) when the bondholder is hit, \(\ell_{t}=0\) otherwise. * Liquidity shock = cash constraint or need to rebalance portfolio. * Liquidity-shock intensity \(\lambda_\ell(y_t)\), defined through: \[ \underbrace{1- \exp[-\lambda_\ell(y_t)]}_{\approx \lambda_\ell(y_t)\mbox{ if small}} = \mathbb{P}(\ell_t = 1|\underline{w_{t-1}},y_t). \] * \(\ell_t\) is assumed not to Granger-cause \(y_t\) (as in Hypothesis 2.1) and not to be priced (as in Hypothesis 2.3). Using the same reasoning as in Slide XXX, this implies in particular that \[ \lambda_\ell^{\mathbb{Q}}(y_t) = \lambda_\ell(y_t), \] where \[ 1- \exp[-\lambda^{\mathbb{Q}}_\ell(y_t)] = \mathbb{Q}(\ell_t = 1|\underline{w_{t-1}},y_t). \]

Example 5.1 (Structural interpretation of XXX)

  • Each offer is a random fraction \(\omega_{i}\) (\(i\in\{1,\ldots,\kappa\}\)) of \(\widetilde{B}_e (t, h)\) (see Eq.;(5.16)). The \(\omega_{i}\)’s are uniformly distributed in \([0,1]\).
  • At \(t+\varepsilon\), the bond is sold to the trader that has offered the highest price.
  • Formally, when \(\ell_{t}=1\), the selling price is: \[ \left(\max_{i\in\{1,\ldots,\kappa\}}\omega_{i}\right)\tilde{P}_{t,h}^{(e)}. \]
  • Conditional on \(\kappa\), the expectation of \(\max_{i}(\omega_{i})\) is \(\kappa/(\kappa+1)\).%

(Because the \(\omega_{i}\) are i.i.d., the c.d.f. of \(\max(\omega_{1},\ldots,\omega_{\kappa})\) is \(x\mapsto F(x)^{\kappa}\), where \(F\) is the c.d.f. of \(\omega_{i}\).) * \(\theta_\ell^{(e)}\), which is the unconditional expectation of \(\max_{i}(\omega_{i})\), is given by: \[\begin{eqnarray*} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{e^{-\varepsilon\gamma^{(e)}}k}{k+1}\frac{\left(\varepsilon\gamma^{(e)}\right)^{k}}{k!} & = & \left[\left(1-e^{-\varepsilon\gamma^{(e)}}\right)\frac{\varepsilon\gamma^{(e)}-1}{\varepsilon\gamma^{(e)}}+e^{-\varepsilon\gamma^{(e)}}\right]\\ & = & g(\varepsilon\gamma^{(e)})\qquad\mbox{(say).} \end{eqnarray*}\] * Function \(g\) is increasing and valued in \([0,1]\). In addition \(\lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}g(x)\rightarrow1\).

  • Pseudo-illiquidity intensity (mimics Eq. (5.8)): \[\begin{eqnarray} \widetilde{\lambda}_{e,\ell, t} \left( y_t, y_{t-1} \right) &=& - \log \left\{ \exp \Big[ - \lambda_{e,\ell} \left( y_t\right) \Big] \right. + \tag{5.15}\\ && \left. \Big( 1 - \exp \Big[ - \lambda_{e,\ell} \left( y_t \right) \Big] \Big) \theta_\ell^{(e)} \right\} \nonumber \end{eqnarray}\]

  • Adjusted pseudo-price for any date \(t\) and maturity \(h\) (with \(\widetilde{B}_e (t, 0) = 1\)) (mimics Eq. (5.9), replace XXX): \(\ell_{t}=1\)), the investor has to exit by selling her bond holdings. She is then limited in the number of traders she can call (see Appendix XXX) and, as a result, get only a fraction of the price she would get in normal-liquidity times (\(\ell_{t}=0\)).

  • Formally, consider a zero-coupon bond whose residual maturity on date \(t\) is \(h\):

  • If entity \(e\) defaults on date \(t\), or if a liquidity shock hits the investor on date \(t\), then the investor recovers: \[ \left(1 - (1 - RR_t^{(e)})d_t^{(e)}\right)\left(1 - (1 - \theta_\ell^{(e)})\ell_t\right)\widetilde{B}_e (t, h). \]

  • Otherwise (from the investor point of view): \[ B_e(t,h) = (1 - d_t^{(e)})(1 - \ell_t)\widetilde{B}_e (t, h). \]

  • If the pseudo-illiquidity intensity \(\widetilde{\lambda}_{\ell, t}\) is an affine function of \(y_t\), Prop. 5.2 can easily be extended to the pricing of defaultable and illiquid bonds.

Example 5.2 (Euro area sovereign credit spreads)

  • A. Monfort and Renne (2011) and Alain Monfort and Renne (2014): credit/liquidity decompositions of euro-area sovereign spreads.

  • Different estimation approaches: C.-J. Kim (1994)’s filter (see XXX) for MR2014, and Kitagawa-Hamilton filter (see XXX) for MR2011.

  • \(w_t = (y_t',z_t')'\) follows a Regime-Switching Gaussian VAR (see Example 3.1).

  • \(z_t\) (homogenous and exogenous Markov chain) = crisis indicator.

  • Specification of the total intensity: \[ \widetilde{\lambda}_{e,t} = \underbrace{\alpha_{e,c}'z_t + \beta_{e,c}'y_t}_{\mbox{credit intensity $\widetilde{\lambda}_{e,c,t}$}} + \underbrace{\alpha_{e,\ell}'z_t + \beta_{e,\ell}'y_t.}_{\mbox{liquidity intensity $\widetilde{\lambda}_{e,\ell,t}$}} \]

  • In both papers, identification of credit/liquidity components based on: \[ \mbox{KfW-Bund spreads} = \mbox{liquidity spreads} \] (KfW bonds fully guaranteed against default by federal government).

  • In MR2011, \(y_t\) is 5-dimensional vector of observed factors:

  • \((y_{1,t},y_{2,t},y_{3,t})' = (level,slope,curvature)'\) of the German (Bund) yield curve.

  • \((y_{4,t},y_{5,t})' =\) PC1 and PC2 of 10-year spreads against Germany (France, Italy, Netherlands and Spain).

  • 2 regimes, one is a crisis regime.

  • Use of surveys in the estimation (see XXX).

  • 2-step estimation approach (estimated factors shown on Figure ??):

  • Historical dynamics of \(w_t\) estimated by Kitagawa-Hamilton (KH) filter (Appendix XXX).

  • \(z_t\) fixed (based on smoothed estimates from KH). Non-linear-least-squares techniques, taking into account the internal-consistency issue. More precisely, we estimate \(\theta\), the vector of parameters defining \(\mathbb{Q}\) dynamics, by: \[ \hat\theta = \underset{\theta}{argmin} \left\{\sum_{e,t,h} (R^{obs}_{e,t,h} - R_{e,t,h}(\theta))^2 \right\} + \chi \left\{\sum_{t,i} (y^{obs}_{i,t} - y_{i,t}(\theta))^2 \right\}, \] where \(\chi\) penalizes inconsistency between observed and model-implied observable factors \(y_t\).

  • In Alain Monfort and Renne (2014), we have \[ \widetilde{\lambda}_{e,t} = \underbrace{y_{e,t}}_{=\widetilde{\lambda}_{e,c,t}} + \underbrace{(1-\theta^{(e)}_\ell)y_{E+1,t}}_{=\widetilde{\lambda}_{e,\ell,t}}, \] hence \(y_t\) of dimension \(E+1 = 12\) (11 countries are involved).
  • 9 regimes: \(z_t = z_{c,t} \otimes z_{\ell,t}\), where \(z_{i,t}\) (\(i \in \{c,\ell\}\)) is 3-dimensional.
  • \(z_{i,t}=[1,0,0]'\): low stress,
  • \(z_{i,t}=[0,1,0]'\): medium stress,

Example 5.3 (IBOR-OIS spreads)

  • Dubecq et al. (2016) decompose the EURIBOR-OIS spreads into a credit and a liquidity component. (OIS: see Def. ??).
  • \(N\) banks of EURIBOR panel (homogeneity assumption).
  • EURIBOR rate = rate of an unsecured loan. From the point of view of bank \(j\), lending to bank \(e\) (\(e,j \in \{1,\dots,N\}\)): \[\begin{equation} R^{IBOR}_{t,h} = - \frac{1}{h} \log \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}_t \left\{ \exp \left[ - \sum_{i=1}^{h} \left( r_{t+i-1} + \widetilde{\lambda}_{e,c, t+i} + \widetilde{\lambda}_{j,\ell, t+i} \right) \right] \right\}.\tag{5.17} \end{equation}\]
  • Overnight-Index Swap (OIS) rate: \[\begin{equation} \Rightarrow R^{OIS}_{t,h} = - \frac{1}{h} \log \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}_t \left\{ \exp \left( - \sum_{i=1}^{h} r_{t+i-1} \right) \right\}.\tag{5.18} \end{equation}\]
  • \(r_t\) assumed to be independent from \(\widetilde{\lambda}'s +\) homogeneity assumption \(\Rightarrow\) \[ R^{IBOR}_{t,h} - R^{OIS}_{t,h} = - \frac{1}{h} \log \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}_t \left\{ \exp \left[ - \sum_{i=1}^{h} \left( \widetilde{\lambda}_{c, t+i} + \widetilde{\lambda}_{\ell, t+i} \right) \right] \right\}. \]
  • Interest-rate swap where the floating rate is an overnight-rate reference (EONIA in the euro area).
  • At maturity, the payoff received by the fixed-rate payer is the difference between:
  1. the notional (\(W\), say) inflated with the date-\(t\) OIS (fixed) rate (i.e. \(W\exp\left\{h R_{t,h}^{OIS}\right\}\)) and
  2. the same notional capitalized with the realized short-term rates (i.e. \(W\exp\left\{r_{t} + \ldots + r_{t+h-1}\right\}\)).
    1. and (b) lead to Eq. (5.18).
    1. and (b) assume that the OIS reference rate \(\equiv\) risk-free rate \(r_t\) \(\Leftrightarrow\) overnight interbank market preserves the lending bank from (i) liquidity and (ii) credit risk. Rationale:
  1. By rolling its cash on the overnight market (at the EONIA rate), a bank is not exposed to the risk of having to liquidate longer-term investments upon the realization of the liquidity shock.
  2. While the EONIA is an unsecured-transaction rate, the extremely-short maturity of these transactions substantially reduces the credit-risk exposure of the lending bank.
  • Quadratic specification for \(\widetilde{\lambda}_{c, t}\) and \(\widetilde{\lambda}_{\ell, t}\): \[ \widetilde{\lambda}_{c, t} = x_{c,t}^2 \quad \mbox{and} \quad \widetilde{\lambda}_{\ell, t} = x_{\ell,t}^2, \] where \(X_t = (x_{c,t},x_{\ell,t})'\) follows a Gaussian VAR(1) process.

\(\Rightarrow\) Quadratic Gaussian framework (see Example 1.8). * \(X_t\) is latent. Estimation by Quadratic Kalman Filter (Alain Monfort, Renne, and Roussellet 2015). Measurement equations include equations stating that observed proxies of credit and liquidity risks relate to quadratic functions of \(x_{c,t}\) and \(x_{\ell,t}\), respectively.

\(\square\) If \(f (y_t | w_{t-1}) = f (y_t | y_{t-1} , d_{1, t-1})\) (entity \(e = 1\) is systemic) then: * The pseudo-price \(\widetilde{B}_e (t, h)\) now depends on \(y_t\) and \(d_{1, t}\); * Eq. \(\square\) If the default event of the first entity only (\(d_{1,t}\)) is a source of risk that is priced: \(M_{t-1, t}( w_t, w_{t-1}) = M_{t-1, t}( y_t, y_{t-1}, d_{1, t}, d_{1, t-1})\), then * \(\lambda^{\mathbb{Q}}_{1, t} \neq \lambda_{1, t}\) and \(\lambda^{\mathbb{Q}}_{2, t} = \lambda_{2, t}\); * the exogeneity of \(\{ y_t \}\) is {} under \(\mathbb{Q}\); * Eq. 5.6 General Affine Credit-Risk Framework

  • Exploiting VARG processes, Alain Monfort et al. (2021) propose a general affine credit-risk pricing model jointly allowing for:
  1. systemic entities (breaking down the no-jump condition; see Collin-Dufresne, Goldstein, and Hugonnier (2004));
  2. contagion effects between entities [economic/financial linkages; see Ait-Sahalia, Laeven, and Pelizzon (2014)];
  3. pricing of credit events (credit spread puzzle; see C. Gourieroux, Monfort, and Renne (2014));
  4. and stochastic recovery rates (RR) (Altman et al. 2005).
  • This general framework is presented in the following.
  • State vector \(w_t\) of the form \([y_t',\delta_t']'\), where \(\delta_t\) is a \(E\)-dimensional vector of {} (see Hypothesis 5.3).

Hypothesis 5.3 (XXX) The default date \(\tau^{(e)}\) (say) of any entity \(e\) is defined as: \[\begin{equation} \tau^{(e)} = \inf \left\{ t > 0 : \delta^{(e)}_t > 0 \right\},\tag{5.19} \end{equation}\] where \(\delta^{(e)}_t\) is a non-negative variable called {}. The default indicator function can be equivalently written as \(d^{(e)}_t = \textbf{1}_{ \{ \tau^{(e)} \leq t \} }\) or \(d^{(e)}_t = 1 - \textbf{1}_{ \{ \underline{\delta^{(e)}_t} ' {\bf 1} = 0 \} }\), with \(\underline{\delta^{(e)}_t} = (\delta^{(e)}_t, \ldots, \delta^{(e)}_1)\) and where \({\bf 1} = (1, \ldots, 1)'\) with conformable dimension.

The historical Laplace transform of \(w_t\), conditional \(\underline{w}_{t-1}\), is denoted by: \[\begin{equation} \begin{array}{lll} \varphi^{\mathbb{P}}_{w, t-1} (u_w) = \mathbb{E} \left[\exp( u_w ' w_t ) | \underline{w_{t-1}} \right] = \exp \left[ a_{w} (u_{w}) ' w_{t-1} + b_{w} (u_{w}) \right] , \end{array}\tag{5.20} \end{equation}\] with \(u_w = (u_y ', u_{\delta} ')'\).

5.8 Top-Down Approach

  • Relaxing the assumptions underlying the classical framework can also be done in the context of top-down approaches.
  • Top-down models focus on default counting (or loss) processes [e.g. Azizpour, Giesecke, and Kim (2011), Giesecke, Goldberg, and Ding (2011), contrary to the approach presented above (bottom-up) that consider default processes of individual firms as the model primitives Darrell Duffie and Garleanu (2001).
  • The top-down approach has been shown to satisfactorily capture the existence of default clustering Errais, Giesecke, and Goldberg (2010).
  • Building on C. Gourieroux, Monfort, and Renne (2014), Christian Gourieroux et al. (2021) propose an affine top-down model consistent with: the presence of systemic entities, contagion, and the pricing of default events.

Example 5.5 (Disastrous Defaults)

  • Model proposed by Christian Gourieroux et al. (2021).
  • \(n^s_{t}\): Number of systemic defaults on date \(t\).
  • \(N^s_{t}\): number of systemic entities in default at date \(t\) (\(N^s_{t}=n^s_{t} + N^s_{t-1}\)).

\(\underline{k_{t}}=\{k_t,k_{t-1},\dots\}\).

  • Conditional distribution of the number of systemic defaults: \[\begin{eqnarray} n^s_{t+1}| \underline{x_{t+1}}, \underline{y_{t+1}}, \underline{N^s_{t}} &\sim& \mathcal{P}oisson(\beta y_{t+1}+c n^s_{t}),\tag{5.35} \end{eqnarray}\] where \(y_t\) is a nonnegative factor fluctuating around a low-frequency component \(x_t\).

  • \((y_t,x_t)'\) follows a VARG dynamics (Eq. (2.17)). This dynamics admits the following VAR representation (using Prop. 1.2): \[\begin{equation} \left\{ \begin{array}{ccl} y_t - x_t &=& \rho_y (y_{t-1} - x_{t-1}) + \sigma_{y,t}\varepsilon_{y,t}\\ x_t - \mu_x &=& \rho_x (x_{t-1} - \mu_x) + \sigma_{x,t}\varepsilon_{x,t}, \end{array} \right.\tag{5.36} \end{equation}\] with \(0<\rho_y<\rho_x<1\).

  • If \(c>0\): Defaults on date \(t\) increases the conditional probability of having additional defaults on the next date \(\Rightarrow\) Systemic defaults are infectious (Davis and Lo 2001}), or contagious.

  • \(\Delta c_t = \log(C_t/C_{t-1})\): Log growth rate of per capita consumption: \[\begin{equation} \Delta c_t = \mu_{c,0} + \mu_{c,x} x_t + \mu_{c,y} y_t + {\mu_{c,z}} z_{t}.\tag{5.37} \end{equation}\] where \(z_t\) depends on systemic defaults: \[\begin{equation} z_t| \underline{x_{t}}, \underline{y_{t}}, \underline{N^s_{t}} \sim \gamma_0(\xi_{z}n^s_{t-1},\mu_z).\tag{5.38} \end{equation}\]

  • \(\gamma_0\) is a distribution featuring a point mass at zero [see Eq. ((1.3) in Example 1.9].

\(\Rightarrow\) The conditional probability that \(z_t=0\) is \(\exp(-\xi_{z}n^s_{t-1})\),

\(z_{t}=0\) as long as there has been no systemic defaults in the previous period, which is rather frequent.

  • If \(\mu_{c,z}<0\) and \(|\mu_{c,z}|\) is large or if \(\mu_{c,z}<0\) and \(|\mu_{c,z}|\) not so large but \(c\) (contamination) is large, then systemic defaults can give rise to disastrous decreases in \(C_t\).
  • Agents feature Epstein-Zin preferences, with a unit elasticity of intertemporal substitution (EIS). An admissible s.d.f. is exponential affine [as in Eq. (2.4)].

\(\Rightarrow\) \(w_t = (x_t,y_t,z_t,n^{s}_t)'\) is affine under \(\mathbb{P}\) and \(\mathbb{Q}\).

  • In this context, a wide range of credit derivatives can be priced. In particular those whose payoffs depend on the default status of the constituents of a reference portfolio: Credit Index swaps (CIS) and synthetic CDO.

  • CDS: protection payoff \(>0\), when the entity on which the CDS is written defaults.

  • CIS: protection payoff \(>0\), when one entity of the underlying portfolio defaults.
