3.1 State-Space Model}

{\(\ref{subsec:Estimation:SSModel}\) – STATE-SPACE MODEL}

* The dynamic asset-pricing models are {\bf\textcolor{blue}{state-space models}}:

The dynamics of all variables, gathered in vector $y_t$ (yields, equity returns, macroeconomic variables, survey-based variables) is accounted for by state variables ($w_t$).
* The equations defining the relationship between the other variables and the state variables are called {\bf\textcolor{blue}{measurement equations}} (Eq.\,\ref{eq:measeq}).
* The equations defining the dynamics of the state variables are called {\bf\textcolor{blue}{transition equations}} (Eq.\,\ref{eq:transeq}).
* If $w_t$ is an affine process, the transition equations admit a VAR(1) representation (see Proposition\,\ref{prop:affineVAR}).


        :::{.definitionLinear State-Space Model]\label{def:LSSM}

            \begin{eqnarray}
                   \underset{(m \times 1)}{y_t}  &=& A + Bw_t + \eta_t  \quad \mbox{with }  \eta_t \sim i.i.d.\,\mathcal{N}(0,\Omega) \label{eq:measeq} \\
                 \underset{(n \times 1)}{w_t} & =& \mu + \Phi w_{t-1} + \Sigma^{\frac{1}{2}}(w_{t-1}) \varepsilon_t,\label{eq:transeq}
            \end{eqnarray}
        where $\varepsilon_t$ is a martingale difference sequence satisfying  $\mathbb{V}ar_t(\varepsilon_{t+1}) = Id$. The components of $\eta_t$ are {\bf\textcolor{blue}{measurement errors}}.
        :::
        \begin{tiny}{\bf Note. } Eq.\,(\ref{eq:transeq}) comes from Proposition\,\ref{prop:affineVAR} (Eq.\,\ref{eq:VARw}).
        \end{tiny}

* There are two different situations:

\item[(a)] all state variables (components of $w_t$) are observed (next slide);
\item[(b)] some of these variables are latent.

* Case (a): One can resort to standard estimation techniques (GMM, Maximum Likelihood) to estimate model parameters.
* Take the maximum likelihood case, we have:
\begin{eqnarray*}
f(y_t,w_t|\underline{y_{t-1}},\underline{w_{t-1}}) &=& f(y_t|\underline{y_{t-1}},\underline{w_{t}}) \times \underbrace{f(w_t|\underline{y_{t-1}},\underline{w_{t-1}})}_{= f(w_t|\underline{w_{t-1}})}\\
&=& \bv{n}(y_t;A + Bw_t,\Omega) f(w_t|\underline{w_{t-1}}),
\end{eqnarray*}
where $\bv{n}(x;\mu,\Omega)$ denotes the evaluation, at vector $x$ of the p.d.f. of the multivariate normal distribution $\bv{n}(\mu,\Omega)$, i.e., if $x$ is a $m$-dimensional vector:

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:varPHI} \mathbf{n}(x;\mu,\Omega) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{(2 \pi)^{m}|\Omega|}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}\{x - \mu\}'\Omega^{-1}\{x-\mu\}\right). \end{equation}\] * Once this conditional p.d.f. is known, the total likelihood is given by (conditional on \(y_0\) and \(w_0\)): \[ \prod_{t=1}^T f(y_t,w_t|\underline{y_{t-1}},\underline{w_{t-1}}). \] * \(f(w_t|\underline{w_{t-1}})\) depends on the process chosen for \(w_t\). If complicated to compute, one can employ Pseudo ML (see next slide).

* In case (b.iii), one can estimate the model by fitting sample moments computed using observed variables (prices, yields, returns) $\Rightarrow$ {\color{blue}Generalized Method of Moments (GMM)}.


In the context of affine processes, conditional and unconditional moments of the state vector $w_t$ are available in closed from (see Eqs.\,\ref{eq:condmean}, \ref{eq:condvar} and \ref{eq:uncondmeanvar}).


If the model-implied moments are not available in closed-form, one may have to to resort to the {\color{blue}Simulated Method of Moments (SMM)} [\href{https://www.econometricsociety.org/publications/econometrica/1993/07/01/simulated-moments-estimation-markov-models-asset-prices}{Duffie and Singleton (1993)} or \href{http://www.oxfordscholarship.com/view/10.1093/0198774753.001.0001/acprof-9780198774754}{Gouri\'eroux and Monfort (1996)}].

%* In case (a), one can also use the method of moments or one can alternatively maximize the likelihood associated with the model.

* In cases (b.i) and (b.ii), one has to implement {\color{blue}filtering methods}.

* We focus on the latter context in the following.

3.2 Pricing Illiquid Bonds}

{\(\ref{subsec:Credit:Illiq}\) – PRICING ILLIQUID BONDS (OR ASSETS)}

* A very similar approach can be used to price illiquid assets.
* The idea consists in replacing the Loss Given Default ($LGD = 1-RR^{(e)}$) by a Loss Given Liquidity Shock ($1-\theta_\ell^{(e)}$).
* Structural interpretation building on \href{http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1540-6261.2006.01056.x/abstract}{Ericsson and Renault (2006)} (see Appendix\,\ref{app:IlliqLGD}).
* Novel binary variable: $\ell_t$ = ``liquidity shocks''

$\ell_{t}=1$ when the bondholder is hit, $\ell_{t}=0$ otherwise.
* Liquidity shock = cash constraint or need to rebalance portfolio.
* {\bf \textcolor{blue}{Liquidity-shock intensity}} $\lambda_\ell(y_t)$, defined through:
$$
\underbrace{1- \exp[-\lambda_\ell(y_t)]}_{\approx \lambda_\ell(y_t)\mbox{ if small}} = \mathbb{P}(\ell_t = 1|\underline{w_{t-1}},y_t).
$$
    * $\ell_t$ is assumed not to Granger-cause $y_t$ (as in Assumption\,\ref{ass:A1}) and not to be priced (as in Assumption\,\ref{ass:A3}). Using the same reasoning as in Slide\,\ref{slide:lambdaPlambdaQ}, this implies in particular that
    $$
    \lambda_\ell^\mathbb{Q}(y_t) = \lambda_\ell(y_t),
    $$
    where
$$
1- \exp[-\lambda^\mathbb{Q}_\ell(y_t)] = \mathbb{Q}(\ell_t = 1|\underline{w_{t-1}},y_t).
$$

%More precisely, the underlying context is the following (): % % * The bondholder is subject to . % * Upon the arrival of the liquidity shock (\(\ell_{t}=1\)), the investor has to exit by selling her bond holdings. % * This liquidation has to be done in a limited period of time, between \(t\) and \(t+\varepsilon\) (where \(\varepsilon<<1\)). % * The expected number of offers \(\varepsilon\gamma^{(e)}\) reflects the liquidity of the bonds issued by debtor \(e\) (next slide): % % The more liquid the bond, the higher \(\gamma^{(e)}\), and the lower \(\theta_\ell^{(e)}\). %

* {\bf \textcolor{blue}{Pseudo-illiquidity intensity}} (mimics Eq.\,\ref{eq:pseudodefintens}):

\[\begin{eqnarray}\label{eq:pseudoilliqintensity} \widetilde{\lambda}_{e,\ell, t} \left( y_t, y_{t-1} \right) &=& - \log \left\{ \exp \Big[ - \lambda_{e,\ell} \left( y_t\right) \Big] \right. +\\ && \left. \Big( 1 - \exp \Big[ - \lambda_{e,\ell} \left( y_t \right) \Big] \Big)\,\theta_\ell^{(e)} \right\} \nonumber \end{eqnarray}\]

  • Adjusted {} for any date \(t\) and maturity \(h\) (with \(\widetilde{B}_e (t, 0) = 1\)) (mimics Eq.,\(\ref{eq:BtildeCredit}\), replace ): \[\begin{equation}\label{eq:pseudopriceilliq} \widetilde{B}_e (t, h) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}_t \left\{ \exp \left[ - \Sum_{i=1}^{h} \left( r_{t+i-1} + \widetilde{\lambda}_{e,c, t+i} + \widetilde{\lambda}_{e,\ell, t+i} \right) \right] \right\}. \end{equation}\]

    • Upon the arrival of the liquidity shock (\(\ell_{t}=1\)), the investor has to exit by selling her bond holdings. She is then limited in the number of traders she can call (see Appendix,\(\ref{app:IlliqLGD}\)) and, as a result, get only a fraction of the price she would get in ``normal-liquidity times’’ (\(\ell_{t}=0\)).

    • Formally, consider a zero-coupon bond whose residual maturity on date \(t\) is \(h\):

      • If entity \(e\) defaults on date \(t\), or if a liquidity shock hits the investor on date \(t\), then the investor recovers: \[ \left(1 - (1 - RR_t^{(e)})d_t^{(e)}\right)\left(1 - (1 - \theta_\ell^{(e)})\ell_t\right)\widetilde{B}_e (t, h). \]
      • Otherwise (from the investor point of view): \[ B_e(t,h) = (1 - d_t^{(e)})(1 - \ell_t)\widetilde{B}_e (t, h). \]

    • If the pseudo-illiquidity intensity \(\widetilde{\lambda}_{\ell, t}\) is an affine function of \(y_t\), Prop.,\(\ref{prop:standardCreditFormula}\) can easily be extended to the pricing of defaultable and illiquid bonds.

3.3 Top-Down Approach}

{\(\ref{subsec:Credit:DD}\) – TOP-DOWN APPROACH}

* Relaxing the assumptions underlying the classical framework can also be done in the context of ``{\bf\textcolor{blue}{top-down}}'' approaches.
* ``Top-down'' models focus on default counting (or loss) processes [e.g. \href{https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0165188911000558}{Azizpour, Giesecke, Baeho (2011)}, \href{https://stanford.box.com/s/fyk0lvl5hsku8a49ep8pj54yjf1uhwxv}{Giesecke, Goldberg, Ding (2011)}], contrary to the approach presented above (``bottom-up'') that consider default processes of individual firms as the model primitives [e.g. \href{https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF01531332}{Lando (1998)}, \href{https://academic.oup.com/rfs/article-abstract/12/4/687/1578719}{Duffie and Singleton (1999)}, \href{https://tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.2469/faj.v57.n1.2418}{Duffie and G\^arleanu, 2001}].
* The ``top-down'' approach has been shown to satisfactorily capture the existence of default clustering [\href{https://www.worldscientific.com/doi/10.1142/S0219024907004342}{Brigo, Pallavicini, Torresetti (2007)}, \href{https://epubs.siam.org/doi/pdf/10.1137/090771272}{Errais, Giesecke and Goldberg, 2010}].
* Building on \href{http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304407614001080}{Gouri\'eroux, Monfort and Renne (2014)}, \href{https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3188085}{Gouri\'eroux, Monfort, Mouabbi and Renne (2018)} propose an affine ``Top-down'' model consistent with: the presence of systemic entities, contagion, and the pricing of default events.

This dynamics admits the following VAR representation (using Prop.,\(\ref{prop:affineVAR}\)): \[\begin{equation}\label{eq:systemF12} \left\{ \begin{array}{ccl} y_t - x_t &=& \rho_y (y_{t-1} - x_{t-1}) + \sigma_{y,t}\varepsilon_{y,t}\\ x_t - \mu_x &=& \rho_x (x_{t-1} - \mu_x) + \sigma_{x,t}\varepsilon_{x,t}, \end{array} \right. \end{equation}\] with \(0<\rho_y<\rho_x<1\).

  • If \(c>0\): Defaults on date \(t\) increases the conditional probability of having additional defaults on the next date \(\Rightarrow\) { Systemic defaults are infectious [], or contagious}.

:::

:::{.example``Disastrous Defaults’’ (cont’d)]

  • \(\Delta c_t = \log(C_t/C_{t-1})\): Log growth rate of per capita consumption: \[\begin{equation}\label{eq:Deltac_rewritten} \Delta c_t = \mu_{c,0} + \mu_{c,x} x_t + \mu_{c,y} y_t + {\mu_{c,z}} z_{t}. \end{equation}\] where \(z_t\) depends on systemic defaults: \[\begin{equation}\label{eq:Zdistri_new} z_t| \underline{x_{t}}, \underline{y_{t}}, \underline{N^s_{t}} \sim \gamma_0(\xi_{z}n^s_{t-1},\mu_z). \end{equation}\]

  • \(\gamma_0\) is a distribution featuring a point mass at zero [see Eq.,(\(\ref{eq:ARG0}\)) in Example,\(\ref{exmpl:ARG1}\)].

    The conditional probability that \(z_t=0\) is \(\exp(-\xi_{z}n^s_{t-1})\),

    \(z_{t}=0\) as long as there has been no systemic defaults in the previous period, which is rather frequent.

  • If \(\mu_{c,z}<0\) and \(|\mu_{c,z}|\) is large or

if \(\mu_{c,z}<0\) and \(|\mu_{c,z}|\) not so large but \(c\) (contamination) is large, then

{ systemic defaults can give rise to ``disastrous’’ decreases in \(C_t\)}.

  • Agents feature {Epstein-Zin preferences}, with a unit elasticity of intertemporal substitution (EIS). An admissible s.d.f. is exponential affine [as in Eq.,(\(\ref{eq:keySDF}\))]. \(w_t = (x_t,y_t,z_t,n^{s}_t)'\) is affine under \(\mathbb{P}\) and \(\mathbb{Q}\).
